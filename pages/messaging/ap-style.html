AP style rules and exceptions

We use Associated Press (AP) style as our default writing style. Below is a quick guide of the AP style rules or exceptions that we use most frequently. For further guidance, you can purchase the full AP Stylebook at apstylebook.com.

Acronyms

Our names should never be made of acronyms as these can be confusing. Instead, be clear and descriptive.

Address format

000 Michigan Ave. Abbreviate Ave., Blvd. and St. and directional cues when used with a numbered address. Always spell out other words such as alley, drive and road. Capitalize and spell out a street name or a directional cue when mentioned without a numbered address.

Commas

When listing items, do not use an Oxford comma (a comma before a conjunction). For exceptions, see the AP Stylebook.

Date format

Monday (Day), June 1 (Month and date), 2020 (Year). Do not use “st” or “th” with date numbers. Abbreviate months Jan., Feb., Aug., Sept., Oct., Nov. and Dec. when used with a specific date, but not when mentioned alone or with only a year. All other months are always spelled out in full.

Em dashes

We prefer to use an em dash instead of the smaller en dash. When using an em dash, our style is to include space on both sides of the dash.

Example: The centerpiece of that mission — of who we are — is that we always have the patient’s best interests in mind.

Healthcare

We always spell healthcare as one word. This is counter to AP style guidance.

Job titles

Do not capitalize job titles within a sentence. Capitalize a person’s job title only when it precedes his or her name or is used on reference collateral such as business cards or email signatures.

Names and medical titles

On first reference, use the subject’s first and last name. In subsequent references, use only the first or last name to refer to the person. Be consistent throughout your piece with whichever you choose. Also, use Dr. on first reference to someone who holds a doctor of dental surgery, medicine, podiatric medicine or veterinary medicine.

Numerals

Use words for one through nine and numerals for numbers 10 and greater. When starting a sentence with a number, always spell out the numeral unless it refers to a year.

Orthopaedic

Orthopaedic and orthopedic are alternate spellings of the same word. We prefer to use orthopaedic.

State names

Spell out a state’s full name when mentioned in the body of a story. Abbreviate state names in datelines, lists, captions and other instances of shortened text using the AP Stylebook’s list of state abbreviations.

Time format

Use lowercase a.m. and p.m. with periods and a space between the number and letters. Minutes are not needed for times on the hour: 1 p.m.

  • When you need to include the time zone, the abbreviations EST, CDT, etc., are acceptable on first reference only when the abbreviation is linked with a clock reading for zones used within the continental U.S.: 6:30 p.m. PST.
  • For zones not used within the contiguous U.S., spell out the time zone even when following a clock reading (e.g., 1 p.m. Alaska Standard Time, 9 a.m. Greenwich Mean Time.) Greenwich Mean Time can be abbreviated as GMT on second reference.
  • Please be mindful of which abbreviation is correct for your time zone during the specific time of year you’re discussing. Time zone names and their corresponding abbreviations change during daylight saving time, which takes place from 2 a.m. on the second Sunday of March until 2 a.m. on the first Sunday of November each year in most areas of the U.S. For example, in Nashville, Tennessee, in August, write 6 p.m. CDT, which stands for Central Daylight Time, but in January, write 6 p.m. CST, meaning Central Standard Time.
